# Interview Company Research

Abstracted from a real GTM-engineering interview prep run. The point is not a generic
company summary. The point is to build tangible artifacts (a trigger tracker, a
competitor-by-surface map, a small live data pull) that double as proof of work, and to
take an honest position on whether the role is worth it.

## Inputs

- Company name (required).
- Role title plus JD text or link (required).
- Optional: deck, website, founder names, any links.

## The research sequence

Run in order. Each step should produce an artifact or a specific finding, not just notes.

1. Ground the company. Search what they do; fetch the site and deck. Define the PRODUCT
   SURFACES, meaning the distinct things they sell. Write the two-sentence sharp-outsider
   description. Sources: company site, deck, news.
2. Define the ICP by job-to-be-done, not by industry label. Cut the parts of the named
   industry that do not actually have the pain. Sources: deck plus reasoning.
3. Size the TAM from public registries, not vibes. Find the authoritative count per
   segment (for example AHA for hospitals, NCES for schools, AHLA for hotels). Give three
   tiers: total addressable, realistic ICP, and the act-now sliver. Flag every estimate.
4. Build the force-function tracker. List the triggers that create urgency: regulation and
   mandates, contract expiries, incidents, and funding events. Verify against PRIMARY
   sources (legislature sites, NCSL, agency data), not vendor blogs. Cross-check the
   highest-stakes list independently. Flag disputed items. This is usually the single most
   differentiating artifact.
5. Map competitors by the company's OWN product surfaces. For each competitor, mark which
   surface they hit and whether they REPLACE or INTEGRATE with the company. Find the
   frenemies (vendors that are both partners and rivals). Sources: competitor sites, case
   studies, job postings, review sites.
6. Map the buying committee. Identify the economic decision-maker versus the champion
   versus the influencers per segment, and the public-data source to reach each (990s,
   board minutes, Clery reports, industry associations, LinkedIn).
7. Map the signal-to-qualification system. If the company publishes a qualification
   framework, use it. Separate the pillars detectable before a call from those earned in
   conversation. Define the signals that predict a qualified opportunity and where each is
   sourced.
8. Pull the scaling comparables. Find three or four companies with the same business-model
   shape; research how they scaled and what moat they built. Do not force a competitor
   where the real story was a fragmented status quo. Extract the transferable lesson.
9. Pressure-test the role against the JD. Take a position: genuine systems ownership or a
   relabeled SDR or analyst seat. State what you would actually own and the honest caveats
   at the company's stage.
10. Run a small live data pull. Pull five to ten real records through the actual free
    pipeline sources for this company's ICP. Surface the flaws at small scale (duplicates,
    junk names, missing fields). This proves you build and QA, not just plan, and becomes a
    talking point.
11. Synthesize the 7-part brief. Flag everything thin or unverified.

## Source playbook (where to actually look)

- Company: site, deck, news, founder LinkedIn.
- TAM: the government or industry registry for each segment.
- Regulation and triggers: NCSL, state legislature sites, law-firm 50-state surveys, agency
  enforcement data. Triangulate. Never trust a single vendor blog.
- Competitors and tech stack: competitor case studies and customer logos, job postings
  (they list the stack), review sites, integrator pages.
- Buyers: IRS Form 990s for nonprofits, board minutes for public bodies, Clery for higher
  ed, industry associations, LinkedIn.
- Live data pull: the relevant free registry plus a free geocoder, before any paid tool.

## Verification discipline

- Prefer primary sources. Treat aggregators as leads, not facts.
- Run an independent cross-check on the highest-stakes list, usually the trigger tracker.
- Every claim that drives a decision needs a source. Flag estimates and inferences.

## Output: the 7-part brief

1. What they do. Two sentences, sharp-outsider voice.
2. Business model. How they actually make money: ACV, contract terms, wedge versus
   recurring, sales motion.
3. Market position. TAM tiers, main competitors by surface, defensible versus commoditized.
4. The role. Genuine systems ownership or a relabeled seat, what you would own, honest
   caveats.
5. Why it is interesting and why it might not be. Tailwinds and pedigree versus risks and
   red flags.
6. Strongest talking points. Each tied to a specific artifact built in the sequence.
7. Sharp questions to ask. Each one provable that you did the work, tied to a real finding.

## Style rules (enforce)

- No em dashes.
- Short declarative sentences.
- Lead each bullet with a concrete specific: a number, a name, a law, or an artifact.
- Flag anything thin or unverified inline, and again in one place at the end.
- Pull only from research actually done. No generic industry filler.

## Quality bar and anti-patterns

- Generic is failure. Every section must contain company-specific specifics.
- Talking points must map to artifacts, not opinions.
- Never invent funding, customer counts, or metrics. If unknown, say so.
- The role verdict must take a position, not hedge.
- The live data pull is mandatory. It is what separates this brief from a wiki summary.
